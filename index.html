Philosophy of Practice

What Law Means to Ritik Jayaswal

I

On Law and Justice

Law is not the same as justice — and an advocate must never confuse the two. The law is the instrument. Justice is the destination. My practice begins with understanding what outcome is truly right for my client, and then working backward through every legal tool available to reach it. The courtroom is not a place of theater. It is a place of accountability. Every argument I make must be able to withstand not just cross-examination, but conscience.

II

On the Indian Legal System

The Indian legal system is one of the most complex and most extraordinary frameworks of justice in the world. It carries the weight of colonial legacy, the ambition of a democratic Constitution, and the daily reality of millions of citizens who need it to work. I believe deeply in this system — not blindly, but with full awareness of where it struggles and where it soars. My role as an advocate is to know it well enough to use it fully, and to use it in a way that moves it, however incrementally, toward the justice it promises.

III

On the Client

When a person walks into a lawyer's chamber, they are almost always carrying fear. Fear of the system, fear of the outcome, fear of being misunderstood. I consider it the first obligation of this practice to remove that fear — not by making false promises, but by making the path clear. A client who understands their case is a client who can participate in it. That participation changes everything. I do not carry cases alone. I carry them with the people whose lives depend on them.
